6. Oruro Carnival

In Bolivia's Oruro, there is a religious and cultural celebration known as Carnival. The 18th century saw the beginning of its celebration. A Christian ritual centered on the Virgin of Candelaria was later incorporated into the celebration, which was once an indigenous holiday. This carnival inspires the display of a wide variety of folk arts every year for six days, including masks, weavings, and embroidery. This year the Oruro Carnival was celebrated from February 21 to February 27. The parade features 10,000 musicians and more than 28,000 dancers. The carnival was not observed in 2021, and Covid-19 claimed the lives of many dancers. Although the festival stimulates the local economy, it also energizes and spreads the local view of the world through syncretism and a strong sense of belonging and collectivity, which is personified in the necessary interaction between artisans, dancers, musicians, and others. Even so, its bearers continue to struggle to keep the festival going.
